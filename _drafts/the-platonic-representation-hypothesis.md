Last week (26th of November 2025) we attended the symposium [From Hype to Reality: AI in the Study of Art and Culture](https://latentcanon.github.io/FromHype2025_program.html#about-section) at the University of Zurich.
It was an interesting event with contribution I would label as *good thinking*.
I am very thankful that we were able to listen and to discuss with people from different disciplines.
Thanks to the organizers.

However, that is actually not what I wanna talk about.
Rather, I mention the symposium because it brought my attention to a very intriguing idea expressed in the paper [The Platonic Representation Hypothesis](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2405.07987) which challenges my *constructivist stance*, that is, my believe that systems construct reality which makes it real.
I currently share this constructivist realist view of Niklas Luhmann.

Of course, I can not refute the hypothesis but I can at least problematise it a little bit more than the authors already did.
I do not disagree with the authors viewpoint or result.
They do not claim anything outlandish.
It is after all only a hypothesis.
However, I think we have to be very careful not to act to quickly on the promise of finding "Truth" or "Reality", especially if such a quest might narrow down the plurality and endless possibility of the *world horizon*.

## Putnam vs. Putnam vs. Putnam

The authors of the paper reference the well-known philosophyer Hilary Putnam and his conception of *Convergent Realism* from 1982 which he was a leading proponent of.
His *No Miracles Argument* is seen as the bedrock of *Convergent Realism*.
So let me summarize it first.

In the 1960s and early 1970s, Putnam was a staunch *Scientific Realist*.
He championed the idea that science is not just a useful tool, but that it actually describes the world as it really is.

>The positive argument for realism is that it is the only philosophy that doesn't make the success of science a miracle.

He argued that if science were just making things up or using "useful fictions", how could we explain that our planes fly, our GPS works, and our medicines cure diseases?
It would be a statistical miracle if our theories were wrong about reality but still worked so perfectly. 
Therefore, scientific theories must be converging (getting closer and closer) on the actual truth of the universe.
This sounds totally reasonable even though it is an argument based on an empirical *observation* of science itself, i.e. if science operates the true/false distinction it produces constantly true as well as false theories (which are often marked as true before).
From a systems theoretical perspective it seems trivial that science is successful in the production of truth.
I come back to this point later.

Around the late 1970s (specifically starting with his 1976 address to the American Philosophical Association), Putnam began to doubt this view. 
He abandoned *Convergent/Scientific Realism* for two main reasons.

### The Graveyard of Truth

First he looked into the history of science and realized that this history is a graveyard of "true" theories.
Newton's gravity was considered "true" for centuries.
Then Einstein came along and showed it was technically "false".
Phlogiston theory (explaining fire) was consiedered "true" but turned out to be totally wrong.
If every theory in the past was eventually proven wrong, statistically speaking, our current theories (quantum mechanics, relativity) will likely be proven wrong in the future, too. 
If that is the case, we cannot claim we are *converging* on a fixed truth. 
**We are just moving from one useful description to another**.

Let me draw your attention to a provocative case: *Animism*.
Today, our established *Ontology of Objects* is being fundamentally challenged by what I term the *Ontology of Relations*. 
The former, foundational to Western thought, originated and developed over the last 2,500 years in European culture, arguably catalyzed by Parmenides when he argued that change (Becoming) is an illusion and that only eternal Being is real.

This Ontology of Objects has been, and probably remains, immensely successful in the natural sciences. 
However, we are also now observing a distinct intellectual shift toward thinking in terms of processes, relations, dynamical systems, and emergence.

This is particularly evident in the study of sustainability, where there is a clear willingness to re-attribute a form of agency to ecosystems, oceans, and other natural processes.
This is not necessarily due to a belief that, for instance, a hurricane consciously decides to destroy a house, but because it offers a **more useful and descriptive framework** for modeling complex, non-linear, and interdependent systems.

In this sense, we are moving toward a modern, sophisticated version of animism and this intellectual trajectory appears to be less of a gradual convergence toward a single attractor and more of a synthesis or a paradigm jump.

These paradigm shifts happened in the past.
It was Thomas Kuhn {% cite kuhn:xxxx %} who discovered that science is more like political regimes.
We have a King (Newtonian Physics). 
It works for a while. 
Then, we find problems. 
We don't just add a brick; we overthrow the King, chop off his head, and install a President (Einsteinian Physics). 
The old rules don't apply anymore.

Kuhn's historical evidence made this very hard to defend the idea that our theories are getting closer and closer to a fixed God's Eye picture of reality.
If Kuhn is right, and science goes through revolutions where we throw out the old concepts entirely (e.g., we threw out Phlogiston entirely; we didn't just improve it), then we aren't converging on anything.
We are just changing lenses.

Putnam looked at Kuhn's arguments and realized that *Convergent Realism* was historically naive.
He couldn't claim we were getting closer to the one true Reality if history shows we keep changing our definition of what reality is.

### Jumping Instead of Converging

Putnam's second argument against himself is a logical one.
He realized that you can describe the same world in infinitely many different ways that are all logically consistent but totally different.

Imagine a room with a chair, a table, and a lamp.
You can have a theory that says "There are 3 objects here".
You can have another theory that counts "atoms" and says "There are trillions of objects here".
You can have another theory that counts "object-parts" (legs, seats, bulbs).
All these theories fit the data. 
All of them work. 
But they contradict each other on what really exists (Is the fundamental object the chair? Or the atom?).

Putnam concluded:

>There is no God’s Eye View" (no single, fixed reality) that we are converging toward.

Instead, truth depends on the *conceptual scheme* (the language game) we are currently using.
This realization birthed *Internal Realism* which to me is compatible to with *Operational Constructivism* of cybernetics and Luhmann's systems theory.

### Internal Realism

Many people read Kuhn and said, 

>Oh, so truth is just whatever the scientists agree on? Truth is just a fashion trend?

This is called (absolute) Relativism.
Putnam was rightfully terrified of (absolute) Relativism. 
He didn't want science to be just fashion.
He agreed with Kuhn that we can't see the world without *conceptual schemes* (paradigms). 
There is no *God's Eye View*.
But he correctly also argued that within our conceptual scheme (our language game), there is still a right and wrong.
We can still have "Truth", but it is an internal human truth, not a mystical cosmic truth.

To understand *Internal Realism*, we have to stop thinking of the world as a finished painting that we are just trying to photograph.
Instead, Putnam wants us to think of the world as dough, and the human mind as a cookie cutter.

Imagine the world comes pre-sliced.
The universe consists of fixed objects (stars, trees, atoms) waiting for us to discover them.
Our job is to make a map that matches these pre-existing borders perfectly.
That is the picture of Metaphysical Realism (the old view).

Now imagine, the world exists, but it is unstructured "stuff" (like a blob of dough).
It doesn't come with lines drawn around "trees" or "atoms."
The mind bring *conceptual schemes* (languages, theories, categories) which act like cookie cutters (very Kantian {% cite kant:1781 %} as well as Brownian {% cite brown:2020 %}).
We press our cutter into the dough. 
Pop! Now we have "objects".
In this situation you cannot ask, "Are there really star-shaped cookies in the dough?" 
The answer is only "Yes" after you use the star-shaped cutter.
This sounds to like constructivism without denying realism, that is, *Operational Constructivism*.

In summary with *Internal Realism* Putnam suggested that we don't create the dough (reality is real), but we create the shapes (the objects/truth). As Putnam famously said: 

>The mind and the world make up the mind and the world.

Putnam used a famous logic puzzle to prove that "Truth" depends on the tool you use. It goes like this:

Imagine a tiny world with just three dots: $$x$$, $$y$$, and $$z$$.
Now, I ask you: "How many objects are in this world?"

+ **Answer A (Standard Math):** There are 3 objects. ($$x, y, z$$).
+ **Answer B (Mereology/Polish Logic):** There are 7 objects. In this system (used by Polish logicians), an "object" is any sum of parts. So you have: $$x, y, z, x+y, x+z, y+z, x+y+z$$.

Who is right?
The Metaphysical Realist panics. 
They think there must be **ONE** true number. God knows if it's 3 or 7!

The Internal Realist (Putnam) is calm. 
He says: "It depends on which cookie cutter you are using."
If you use the standard cutter, 3 is the true answer.
If you use the Polish cutter, 7 is the true answer.
Again, there is no God's Eye View to decide between them. 
Truth is internal to the system you are using.

If there is no *God's Eye View*, does that mean we can just make things up? (e.g., "The moon is made of cheese")?
We can probably all agree that this would be bad.
Relativism is not equal to absolute Relativism where anything goes and I imagine that relativists are tired of pointing this out over and over again.

In Putnam this is where the *Realism* part comes in. 
Putnam argues that Truth isn't just opinion.
Truth is *idealized* *rational* acceptability meaning it fits the evidence we have (rational) and it is what we would believe if we had perfect conditions (idealized).

Imagine we are trying to solve a crime.
Metaphysical realists would claim that the truth is what actually happened, even if all evidence is destroyed.
Absolute relativists would say "The truth is whatever the jury decides today" and internal realists say "The truth is the verdict a perfect detective would reach if they had infinite time, every scrap of DNA, every camera angle, and zero bias".

We might never be that perfect detective, but that ideal is what we aim for. That is our truth.

Later in his life, Putnam looked back at his *Internal Realism* and panicked. 
He believed he had accidentally trapped himself in *Solipsism* which would end any discussion about anything.
Originally he used the following formula to define truth:

>Statement S is true if and only if believing S is justified if epistemic conditions are good enough.

But later he hated this because he worried that if "Truth" equals "My ability to verify it", then reality depends entirely on his mind. 
If I can't verify you exist, do you stop existing?
This relates to the famous *Brain in a Vat* problem---if truth is just internal verification, how do I know I'm not just a brain in a jar hallucinating everything? 
This is *Solipsism*.

### Natural Realism

After *Internal Realism*, Putnam shifted again to a view he called *Natural Realism* (sometimes also called Common Sense Realism or Direct Realism).
As mentioned he thought that by defining truth as *internal verification*, he had cut the cord between the mind and the real world.

The biggest change in Natural Realism is the rejection of the interface conception of the mind. 
Putnam (inspired by William James and Wittgenstein) argued: There is no screen. 
The mind is not a room; the mind is simply an ability to interact with the world. 
When you see an apple, you aren't seeing an image of an apple in your brain; you are seeing the apple itself.

Natural Realism is an attempt to stop doing complex metaphysics and return to common sense.
It states that we perceive objects directly.
We don't perceive *representations* of objects. 
The mind hooks directly onto reality.
We cannot separate mind, body, and world.
They are not three layers (like a sandwich) where the mind tries to reach the world through the body. 
They are twisted together like a rope. 
You cannot have a mind without a world to think about.
We don't shape the dough.
The objects are already there.
Our senses are just open windows that let us see them.

### A Pragmatist Defence of Internal Realism

I think we can always and in any case make the radical sceptical move and argue that everything is a simulation; that nothing is real; that, in fact, God is playing a game with me.
Therefore, I find Putnam's argument against himself rather weak.
It undervalues the social which is necessary for language to develop.
As Wittgenstein argued: 

>There is no private language.

We don't verify reality alone in our heads.
We verify reality as part of a community. 
We play a game called language or to use Maturana's expression *languaging*.
In the game of English, German (or any language), we have an unwritten rule: We assume things are true if we have good evidence for them.

>Before the practice of giving and asking for reasons developed, the noises hominids made to each other did not convey information in any more interesting sense than that in which the motion of ambient molecules conveys information to a themostat, or the digestive enzymes convey information to the contents of the stomach. -- {% cite rorty:2016 %}

But Putnam thinks he needs to prove why the rules of the game exist.
I think we don't need to prove the rules; we just play by them.
In tennis, for example, the rule is "the ball must land inside the line". Putnam is acting like a player stopping the match to ask, "But does the line really exist outside of my perception of the line?"
Let's just keep playing tennis. The rule works because we all agree to play by it.

>Like the beavers' collaboration in getting the dam built, it is a social practice. It was initiated by the novel suggestion that we might use noises rather than physical compulsion to get other humans to cooperate with us. That suggestion gave rise to language. Rationality, thought, and cognition all began when language did. Language gets off the ground not by people giving names to things they were already thinking about but by proto-humans using noises in innovative ways, just as the proto-beavers get the practice of building damns off the ground by using sticks and mud in innovative ways. -- {% cite rorty:2016 %}

This is a pragmatist solution to Putnam's doubts *Internal Realism*.
On this view *Internal Realism* is not *Solipsism*.
It is not about "me"; not about what I (Putnam) can verify inside my head.
Instead it is about the shared standards of evidence we use as a society; it is about "us".
The statement "It is true if conditions are good enough" is not a math equation we need to solve. 
It is a practical tool we use to navigate the world.

## Operational Constructivism

In *Cognition as Construction* {% cite luhmann:1988 %}, Luhmann described his own position which is radical epistomenological.
He starts by ironically connecting himself to *Radical Constructivism* refering to it as "the latest fashion of theories of cognition" doubting that this theory, for the first time, has solved the problem of cognition.
He then refers to Kant's Idealism who asked the reasonable question:

>How can cognition determine objects outside itself? Or: How can cognition determine that there is something independently from itself existing, if everything that cognition determines already presumes cognitive capacity (Erkenntnisleistung) and cannot identify independent from cognition through cognition?

Why is cognition possible even though it has no access to reality that is independent from cognition itself?
Luhmann identifies that radical constructivists turn the "why" into a "because", that is, cognition is only possible because it has no access to reality---because it (which changes to the system's environment) does not constantly interrupt the operations of the system.
He agrees with such a move but insists to look closer and to see what really follows from such a radical move. 

He proposes to exchange the subject/object-difference for his system/environment-difference such that one can question about the closure and differentiation (Ausdifferenzierung) of systems and how such a differentiation replace the premise of a common world by a theory of observation of observing systems, that is, **second-order cybernetics**.

Luhmann starts axiomatic:

>We assume that cognitive systems are real in a real environment, meaning that they exist. Many say such an assumption is naiv. But how shall we shall we begin if not naiv? A reflexion of the beginning can not be at the beginning but can only executed within a theory that has reached a certain complexity.

We can connect this statement to Putnam's doubts about his own Internal Realism. He thought: "How can I prove my theory of verification (the verification of others is only an observed verification that I verify), on the ground of the very same theory". Luhmann answer would probably be that you cannot do this but if your theory is consistent you should be able to make the paradox of verifying your theory of verification productive.
In other words: Luhmann builds a selfreferential circle by constructing a onstructivist theory (it can not stand "outside") that presupposes itself.
He was not afraid of these circle but believed that they are unavoidable.

Since clousure is necessary one look at the conditions of such clousure, e.g., a cell, a brain, a mind, or a communication system.
This eliminates arbitrariness of possible operations.
The opposite it the caes: any observer of a *towards* cognition closed system (note this does not mean cognitively closed!) can observe sharp limitations of what is possible.

>Observe the system that you allege arbitrariness. You will see that your conjecture is wrong. [...] Observe the observer!

Luhmann brings the concept of autopoiesis into play as a condition for closure.
No system can operate outside its own boundaries; this applies even to a cognitive (erkennendes) system.
Luhmann defines the concept of "cognition" more narrowly than Maturana, for example, basing it on the act of distinguishing and designating {% cite brown:1969 %}.

Cognition is manufactured through operations of observing and recording those observations (describing). 
Naturally, this includes observations of observations and descriptions of descriptions. 
These records can also be of a biological or chemical nature. 
With Luhmann, we must deliberately distance ourselves from the anthropomorphization of our vocabulary.

The environment contains no distinctions.
It is as it is. 
It contains no "otherness," no possibilities. 
An observer may determine that there are other observers in the environment, but only if they distinguish between them and what they observe (or distinct environmental events).
All observation is the observer's own achievement. 
Therefore, systems exist only through cognitive achievement, which in turn presupposes the existence of systems!

>We must assume that systems exist because we can only recognize that systems exist within systems.

### The Paradox of the "World"

Luhmann follows George Spencer-Brown by establishing that one cannot designate anything without distinguishing it.
When you say "table," you must distinguish the table from the "non-table" (the rest of the room). Without this boundary drawing, the word has no meaning.

However, with words like "World" or "Reality", a problem arises---one recognized by contemporary critic of constructivism Markus Gabriel {% cite gabriel:2013 %}: Normally, by "World," we mean everything that is. 
But to use the word "World", we must distinguish it as a concept from something else (otherwise, it would just be nameless noise). 
This is the paradox: We use a distinction to designate something that technically should have no "outside" (since there is nothing outside the "World").

What, then, is the "World"?
According to Luhmann, the "World" is neither inside nor outside.
The World is the coin itself.
It is the unity of the difference between system and environment; the bracket that makes both sides possible. 
We use words like "Reality" in a frantic attempt to name both sides of the coin simultaneously---even though linguistically, we can only name one side after the other.
We give the "Whole" a name (Spirit/World), fully aware that we can never see this "Whole" directly because we are always part of it.

### Truth and the Binary Code

Luhmann emphasizes that autopoietic systems operate indifferently to truth.
Your brain (the neurophysiological system) is not interested in truth.
It is only interested in whether neurons fire or not.
An electrical impulse is not true or false; it simply is.
The system runs chemically/electrically, and is therefore indifferent.

However, if the brain merely fires away, no cognition arises, only noise.
Therefore, a structure must be introduced that says: "We will now sort this into the box TRUE and that into the box UNTRUE."

It is not a human, a subject, or a God that provides this structure. Instead, evolution has imposed this code. Systems that could not distinguish between "favorable for me" (true/useful) and "unfavorable for me" (untrue/dangerous) went extinct. The code "True/Untrue" (or historically: useful/harmful) is a successful evolutionary model. It prevailed. It is forced upon the system by its own history of survival. In other words: The system imposes the code upon itself because it "realizes" it cannot treat everything as equal. The system develops a structure (the True/Untrue code), and from that moment on, this structure forces every new operation (every thought, every sentence) to submit to this code.

The distinction becomes a structural necessity, and we should stop looking for a "culprit". The code is a mechanism for reducing complexity. It is imposed so that the system does not suffocate in the chaos of its own possibilities (the indifference of neuronal flashes).

### Truth as Communication

For Luhmann (similar in a sense to Tarski), the difference between "A is" and "It is true that A is" represents a shift in the order of observation from first to second order. "It is true that A is" says nothing new about A, but it says something about the status of the statement. It marks the statement as connectable (anschlussfähig), verified, or valid.

By this, Luhmann intends to express that truth is not a property of the world. Truth is a property of communication about the world. In the world, there is no truth or untruth, only existence and experience. Only the observer, by applying the True/Untrue code, can retrospectively mark truth upon the observation of their environment. The second sentence arises only because we have switched from experiencing to observing the experience. We are observing our own cognitive operation.

### The External Observer: Cybernetics vs. Sociology

Luhmann and cybernetics then make a crucial distinction: When can we assume an external observer? Only provided that there is merely a trivial self-reference---meaning the observer acknowledges the conditions they are subject to (like the cognition they observe) but is not themselves part of the system they are observing!

A brain researcher must admit they are neither an angel nor a God. They too have a brain. They too need oxygen. Their eyes also function according to biological laws. But: This does not really disturb their research on another person's brain. They simply say: "Yes, sure, I am also a biological being, but right now I am examining this other being over there."

For sociology, this no longer holds. Why not? When Luhmann, as a sociologist, investigates society (communication), he does so by writing books and holding lectures. But writing books and holding lectures **IS** communication. This means: The sociologist is part of the system they are investigating. They cannot step in front of the glass like the brain researcher and say: "In there is society, and out here is me." As soon as they open their mouth to speak about society, they are performing society.

### Science as a Social System

What remains to be discussed is science. Here, Luhmann helps us understand that within science (and society in general), cognition is not a psychological process at all. Psychic systems are the environment for science.

What we think in our heads is irrelevant to science as long as it stays there. Thoughts are intransparent. No one can look into our heads. Therefore, cognition only takes place when it is communicated. The consequence: Truth is not a state of neurons, but a symbol in communication. Only sentences can be true or untrue, not thoughts. Science is a social system consisting of publications, lectures, and citations and not of people thinking. The human being is merely environment to the science system.

Truth is then a symbolically generalized communication medium. It is not a property of the world out there, but a symbol carried along in communication. It serves to mark a selection (a claim) in such a way that others accept it without having to verify it themselves. When science marks something as "true", it communicates that this specific communicative offering has been tested according to the rules of science, so that science can continue to work with it.

Everything that happens in the science system---the programs, i.e., methods and theories---is continuously subjected to the true/false code. The system is not interested in whether the sentence truly hits "reality" (no one knows that). It is only interested in whether the sentence can be assigned to the code. If a sentence is neither true nor false (e.g., "This sentence tastes delicious"), it is ejected from the science system. While the code is constant, the programs can change; thus, for Luhmann, truth is also dependent on time and method. The system is driven by its autopoiesis, that is, the continuation of communication.

>Truth is the invention that makes it possible to be mistaken without communication breaking down. -- Luhmann

Truth, for Luhmann, is not a triumph of cognition over reality, but an internal achievement of the system to bring order to its own statements. It is an ordering mechanism for quantities of text, not for the world.

### Resistance and Reality

Arbitrariness is avoided because reality offers resistance, yet---unlike in Kant---this resistance is relocated into the system. Nevertheless, reality remains that which disturbs, that which disappoints expectations. If the system constructs "I can fly" and jumps off the roof, reality registers resistance through the hard impact. The system (the body) dies or learns from it. Cognition is thus the recasting of reality-resistances into information. We only learn when things do not go as thought.

As mentioned, with Luhmann, resistance is relocated into the system. The system only checks itself. This raises the question: Why doesn't the system simply "edit out" this resistance? When you kick a stone, the pain is an event in your own nerves (internal), and the surprise that your foot stops is an event in your expectations (internal). The resistance is thus the internal processing of an irritation.

**HOWEVER:** In the evolution of life (and society), only those systems have stabilized whose internal constructions worked (were viable). The system cannot abolish the resistance because this resistance has become an *Eigenvalue* of the system. It has become ingrained. The system constantly checks: "If I think A, does B follow?" If reality says "No" (resistance), and the system ignores this "No", the internal order collapses. The system would produce nothing but noise.

The system constantly designs possibilities (constructions) and tests these constructions. Reality (the environment) cannot dictate what the system thinks. But it can destroy or block.

### Operational Closure and the Blind Spot

The cause may be outside, but the effect is always inside. There are causal relationships between inside and outside---Luhmann does not deny this---but operatively, the system is closed. Yes, there is "something" out there. A highly complex, interconnected reality (atoms, energy, waves). But this reality has no properties we could understand. The reality outside does not know the words "wall", "hard", or "resistance". Outside, there is only noise (chaos, energy). Resistance is not a property of the wall, for instance, but a property of my interaction with it. Another system would construct the same external stimulus completely differently. The environment cannot instruct the system. One could say:

>Resistance is not the shackle of the system, but the scaffolding along which it climbs.

A final important concept is the blind spot of observation. Normally, we assume that a blind spot is an error. The system is missing something or not functioning correctly, otherwise, it would be able to observe everything. Luhmann instead adopts Maturana's insight that the blind spot is the condition for systems to be able to observe anything at all.

Observing is distinction and indication. The system must draw a boundary (distinction) and mark one of the two sides (indication). For logical reasons, the system cannot observe the drawing of the boundary and perform it at the same time. In other words: As long as a system is busy observing the content (the indication), the tool with which it observes (the distinction) is invisible to the system.

During observation, the result of the distinction is manifest. We look at another person and think: "I find them likeable." This judgment is manifest. In the background (latency) lies the distinction (the schema) the system used without realizing it. Why was this specific distinction chosen and not another? The other side could also have been marked. Every observation cuts the world in two: into the marked space (the inside)---what one thinks about, talks about, communicates--and the unmarked space, everything else that is currently ignored in order to move forward.

When a biologist examines a frog (observation), their blind spot includes the beauty of the frog, the market value of the frog, or the religious significance of the frog. As a biologist, they cannot see this; otherwise, they would no longer be a biologist. Their blindness is what makes them see. If they were not blind to religion, they could not conduct biological research. To see the blind spot, according to Luhmann, one needs second-order observation, which, however, creates a new blind spot.

Three essential points follow from this:

**(1) Rejection of the Claim to Absoluteness:** If every cognition has a blind spot, no one (neither Pope nor science nor politics) can claim to possess the "whole truth." Every truth is relative to the chosen distinction.

**(2) Construction of Reality:** Systems construct their world through the choice of their distinctions. Someone who sees the world through "Friend/Enemy" (politics) lives in a completely different constructed reality than someone who sees it through "Have/Have-not" (economy).

**(3) The Impossibility of the "View from Nowhere":** There is no "God's eye view." One cannot see the world "as a whole." One must always stand at a specific point and wear specific glasses. And one cannot watch oneself wearing the glasses.

## The Platonic Representation Hypothesis

The *Platonic Representation Hypothesis (PRH)* posits that neural networks, despite varying architectures, training objectives, and data modalities, are not merely learning disjoint probability solution of a certain domain. 
Instead, they are converging toward a singular, shared statistical model of Reality.

The central tenet of the PRH is that as artificial intelligence systems scale in size and data diversity, their internal representations are becoming increasingly homogeneous.
The authors argue that this convergence is non-accidental; it is driven by the pressure to model the joint distribution of events in the *underlying world*, denoted as $$Z$$.

Drawing directly from Plato's *Allegory of the Cave*, the hypothesis conceptualizes our training data---whether images ($$X$$) or text ($$Y$$)---as mere "shadows" or projections of this underlying reality ($$Z$$).
While the projections differ in modality, they are causally linked to the same *latent physical processes*.
Consequently, sufficiently powerful representation learning algorithms, when optimizing for general competence, will inevitably align their internal vector spaces to capture the structure of $$Z$$.

### The Drivers of Convergence

The authors identify three primary mechanisms driving models toward this *Platonic ideal*:

**(1) Multitask Scaling:** As models are tasked with solving a broader range of problems (multitask learning), the set of viable representations that satisfy all constraints shrinks.
This is framed as the *Anna Karenina scenario*: 

>All strong models are alike; each weak model is weak in its own way.

**(2) Capacity:** Larger hypothesis spaces (larger models) are more likely to cover the optimal representation of reality.
As capacity increases, models with different initializations or architectures settle into the same basin of solutions that best approximate the global optimum.

**(3) Simplicity Bias:** Deep networks exhibit an implicit bias toward simple functions. 
The authors argue that since the fundamental laws of nature are likely simple, this inductive bias steers models toward the true physical solution rather than overly complex, idiosyncratic alternatives.

### Empirical Evidence

The hypothesis is supported by observing the *kernel alignment* between distinct models. 
The authors demonstrate that as vision models and large language models (LLMs) increase in performance, the similarity of their internal similarity structures (their kernels) increases linearly.

Crucially, this alignment crosses the **modality gap**.
A vision model observing a red sphere and a language model processing the text "red sphere" tend to organize these datapoints with similar relative distances in their respective embedding spaces.
The PRH interprets this as evidence that both models are piercing through the veil of their specific sensory modalities to recover the isomorphic structure of the shared world $$Z$$.

## Why does it matter?

If the *Platonic Representation Hypothesis* holds true, the implications for science, philosophy, and engineering are nothing short of revolutionary.
It moves the discussion of AI scaling laws from "better performance" to "epistemological truth".
So, here is why this trend matters:

**(1) Vindication of Convergent Realism:** For centuries, philosophers have debated whether our scientific models actually touch reality or merely produce useful fictions.
The PRH suggests the former. 
The authors explicitly align their findings with "convergent realism"---the idea that successive scientific theories (and now, neural networks) are getting closer to a true, mind-independent reality.
If AI models trained on radically different data (e.g., pure text vs. pure pixels) independently discover the exact same statistical structures, it strongly suggests that an objective reality ($$Z$$) exists and is accessible to *observation*.

**(2) The "Anna Karenina" Scenario for Intelligence:** If all strong models are alike, each weak model is weak in its own way.
This implies that model diversity is a temporary artifact of immaturity. 
As models scale, arbitrary differences in architecture or training objectives fade away, replaced by a singular, optimal representation of the world. 
In this view, sufficiently advanced AIs will not have "personalities" or "perspectives"---they will have agreement.
In the end, Plato and Hegel were right while Nietzsche and Rorty were wrong.

**(3) The Universal Translator:** If the hypothesis is correct, the boundaries between data modalities are artificial. 
The paper suggests that representations are converging to the point where translation between vision, language, and audio becomes trivial. 
Because the underlying geometric structure of a "dog" in a text model aligns with a dog in a vision model, we are approaching a *modality-agnostic era of AI*. 
This implies we can build a single objective model---a true foundation model---capable of processing any sensor data because it understands the common reality generating that data.

**(4) A Solution to Hallucination?** Perhaps most practically, the hypothesis offers a roadmap for reliability. 
If convergence is driven by the statistical structure of reality, then errors and hallucinations are merely symptoms of insufficient scale or data. 
The authors propose that as alignment with $$Z$$ increases, the propensity for models to "make things up" should decrease, as they are increasingly constrained by the actual laws and statistics of the physical world.

**(5) The End of Architecture Engineering:** The hypothesis implies that the specific design of a neural network matters less than we think. 
The paper notes that *scale is all you need* to force convergence, regardless of the specific architecture or objective function. 
This supports the "hardware lottery" view: successful models are simply those that run efficiently on current hardware, but eventually, any sufficiently high-capacity model will be forced by the data to learn the same representation.

**(6) The Simplicity Bias of Reality:** The authors propose is that reality itself is simple, following in line with Occam's razor. 
They argue that deep networks have an inductive bias toward simple functions, and because they are converging on reality, the fundamental laws of nature ($$Z$$) must also be simple functions.
This is a strong metaphysical claim: that the "code" of the universe aligns with the inductive biases of gradient descent.

## Can we Ignore the Politics?

From a science point of view we should ignore anything execpt the science, i.e. we should operate in the true/false code.
However, in a blog post I allow myself of expressing opinions.
Thus it is hard to ignore the institutional context from which the *Platonic Representation Hypothesis* emerges. 

The lead author, Minyoung Huh, is a researcher at OpenAI (specifically contributing to GPT-5 and future reasoning models), and senior author Phillip Isola (MIT) has previously served as a Visiting Research Scientist at OpenAI.
Now these two researchers are surely excellent in what they do and when doing science they will probably work with care and rigor.
They are not to blame that scientific institutions rely on big tech companies to do research on the most sophisticated AI models.

The coupling between the economy and science is a little strict these days.
While scientific hypotheses should be judged on their merit, a systems theory perspective requires us to look at the function of this theory within the system of AI production.
The PRH is not merely a descriptive observation and a very interesting suggestion; it also acts as a normative justification for the specific engineering strategy currently being bet on by OpenAI---a company that appears to be too big to fail.

If the PRH is true, then building specialized models for different tasks seems, at best, a short-lived alternative and at worst a waste of money and time.
If all modalities (vision, text) eventually converge to the same statistical reality, then the most efficient path to *Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)* (whatever that should be) is to stuff everything into one big model.
Thus, the hypothesis conveniently frames OpenAI's engineering constraints (the need for massive, centralized scaling) as a fundamental law of nature.
It transforms a commercial design choice (monolithic architecture) into an ontological inevitability.

The paper explicitly links convergence to scale.
It argues that larger models are "more Platonic" (closer to reality) than smaller ones.
This creates a narrative where **bigger** does not just mean more expensive or more memory-intensive---it means **truer**.
This effectively disqualifies smaller, open-source, or specialized models from the race for "truth" or *viability*.
It suggests that only organizations with the capital to train the largest possible models (e.g., OpenAI, Google, Anthropic) have access to "objective reality".
It frames the resource-heavy approach of Big Tech as the only path to scientific validity.

By framing the convergence of their models as an alignment with an external, objective $$Z$$ (Reality), the authors are effectively saying: 

>Our models agree with each other not because we trained them on similar internet data with similar loss functions, but because they have seen the Truth.

In summary the *Platonic Representation Hypothesis* serves a dual function.
Scientifically, it explores representation alignment. 
But politically, it serves as a legitimation possible myth for the era of the *Foundation Model*. 
It tells investors and the public that the centralization of AI is not a corporate monopoly, but a metaphysical destiny.

Of course, the authors are intereted in research and can most likely seperate their scientific efforts and success from the economic success of OpenAI.
Only because I am worried that such a hypothesis might be damaging for society does not make it false.

## Incompatibilty with Systems Theory

Let me now take on my systems theoretical stance.
I will argue that the PRH is false under this assumption.

### Constructions over Access

The authors hypothesize that distinct models ($$X$$ and $$Y$$) are converging because they are essentially solving for a common variable $$Z$$---the *underlying reality*. 
They assume $$Z$$ pre-exists the observation and that models act as shadow readers in Plato's cave, gradually recovering the actual world outside.

While the Platonic Representation Hypothesis is empirically fascinating, from the perspective of systems theory and second-order cybernetics, it commits a fundamental epistemological error.
It confuses *stability* with *truth*.

The authors observe that models are converging and conclude they are converging on reality ($$Z$$). However, **if we view these neural networks as operationally closed systems**, a different explanation emerges: they are not converging on the world; they are converging on a stable *Eigenbehavior* within the closed loop of human communication.

The PRH assumes a channel of information flow from reality 

$$Z \to \text{Data} \to \text{Model}(X, Y).$$

It presumes the model is observing the world.
If artificial neural networks (ANNs) are operationally closed, they do not interact with the physical world; they interact only with their own internal states (activations, weights, loss functions).
The environment for an ANN is not the physical universe; it is the training data.
Crucially, this training data is not "reality"---it is a **record of human observations**. 
It is text written by humans and images taken by humans.
Therefore, the models are not learning about $$Z$$ (the apple). 
They are learning about the distinctions humans make about $$Z$$ (the word "apple", the camera angle of an apple).
**They are structurally coupled to society**, that is communication, not nature.

### Convergence as Eigenbehavior

Why, then, do the models agree? 
The authors say, 

>Because reality forces them to. 

Heinz von Foerster offers a better explanation: *Eigenvalues*. 
In any recursive system (like training a network over epochs), operations eventually settle into stable states---repeating patterns that do not change under further operations.
Von Foerster famously noted that 

>Eigenvalues represent the equilibria of the system [...] they appear to be 'objects' to the observer.

The *Platonic reality* the authors see is simply the *Eigenbehavior* of the optimization process.
The models are converging because they are essentially solving the same mathematical problem (minimizing loss on human data distributions), not because they have touched the noumenal world---to use a Kantian term.
The shared statistical model is not a model of the world; **it is a model of the invariant structures of human perception**.

### Objectivity vs. Inter-system Consistency

The authors cite the alignment between vision and text models as proof of an underlying objective reality.
However, this is not objectivity; it is **inter-systemic consistency**.
Human language (text) and human vision (images) are already deeply structurally coupled. 
We write about what we see; we take photos of what we can name.

However it is still very surprising and interesting that a model trained on text and a model trained on images, i.e. different modalities, converge!
But---from a constructivist perspective---it does not converge to the real reality as a pragmatist like {% cite rorty:2016 %} would say.
Rather it converges to a statistical model of words and images useage that is coherent with what it observes in the training data, that is, the use of images and words a certain demographic realize to get along with each other.

Both models map the same social reality---the specific way Homo sapiens slices up the continuous complexity of the world into discrete objects.
The models are not finding *Plato's Ideas*; they are reconstructing the social taxonomy of the 21st century of a certian demographic.

### The Observer Problem

The most damning critique is that the PRH ignores the **observer**.
Looking at the helpful illustration one might ask: but who or what is observing the model which observes "reality"?
The PRH works in the mode of first-order observation: The model sees the red sphere.
But, in the cybernetics view, this observation is an observation of a psychic or social system: The model observes how psychic, social (or even other artificial) systems label 'red spheres'.

The PRH claims to eliminate the bias of the observer to find the objective $$Z$$.
But you cannot eliminate the observer. 
The data is the observer's output.

Therefore, the *Platonic representation* is, in fact, an observed sociological construction.
If the model is an artifical system, that is, autopoietic and operationally closed, then all the other social and psychic systems are in its environment.
In this case, it may be very well a perfect, mathematical mirror of its *Umwelt* consisting of psychic and social systems with all their biases, focus, selections, and their specific way of ignoring the rest of the world.

### What if AI is Allopoietic?

A rigid systems theorist might object: 

>Neural networks are not autopoietic; they are *allopoietic* tools created by engineers.

An autopoietic system produces itself (like a cell repairing its own wall, or society producing new communications from old ones).
AI models are built, trained, and updated by engineers. 
They do not currently have the capacity to reproduce themselves or define their own boundaries without human intervention. 
They are trivial machines in the sense that they are tools used within a social system.

Strictly speaking, observers in the environment of a system can only infer that it is actually autopoietic!
However, as far as we know, ANNs are probably not (yet) autopoietic.
A GPT-4 model does not metabolize; it does not reproduce.

However, for the purpose of the Platonic Representation Hypothesis, this distinction makes my argument even stronger.

If the model is merely an allopoietic tool, it is entirely defined by the inputs and goals of its creators (the training data and the loss function).
It has zero independent capacity to access the outside world ($$Z$$).
It is strictly a processor of the system's internal records (data)---The feedback loop 

```
social data -> model update -> model output -> social evaluation -> social data
```

is closed. The entire loop remains inside the boundaries of symbolic processing. 
It never crosses the boundary into the physical environment ($$Z$$).

Therefore, the claim that this tool is "converging on reality" is analogous to claiming that a library catalog is *converging on the books* physically. 
The catalog (the model) is becoming a better map of the library (the data), but it remains forever trapped in the domain of descriptions, never touching the domain of objects.

Whether the system is *autopoietic* or *allopoietic*, the result is the same: Closure. 
The system operates only on distinctions provided by the social system (data), not on the physical world itself.

ANNs beginning to structural couple with society!
Luhmann discusses *technical artifacts* as things that society uses to stabilize expectations.
The convergence is not the AI waking up; 
it is society converging with itself using the AI as a mirror.
ANNs or more generally, data-driven algorithms are **second-order observation machines**.
They automate the observation of how psychic and social systems observe.
