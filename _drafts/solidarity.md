This essay links the thoughts of Richard Rorty and Humberto R. Maturana to demonstrate how the very renunciation of absolute claims to truth can pave the way for a responsible, empathetic, and solidary coexistence. It is a plea not to discover what we have in common in nature, but to actively create it through language and compassion.

## A New Form of Cruelty

Media discourses---whether in traditional mainstream media or the echo chambers of social networks---are currently characterized by the observation of an "attack on democracy". In this context, it is often overlooked that democracy primarily represents a form of political organization of nation-states, while the societies within these states are by no means consistently structured democratically. Companies mostly follow hierarchical logics, and our everyday interactions are far less characterized by consensus than by conflicting interests and contradictions.

What correlates with this supposed attack on institutions, however, is a much deeper cultural phenomenon: a new *glorification of cruelty*. Tech icons like Elon Musk no longer stylize empathy as a social virtue, but instead discredit it as a "means for the suicide of Western civilization".

This development is flanked by the so-called manosphere---a loose network of subcultures that define their identity primarily through the construction of a common enemy: feminism. The demand that men must "finally be men again" reveals a reactionary longing. Hegelianly, this could be interpreted as an antithesis to feminism; as a violent reaction to the increasing sensitization to cruelty towards the "other". The inherent contradiction inevitably leads to the paradox of tolerance: if we demand tolerance in the name of freedom, the radical question arises as to whether we must exclude the intolerance of those who brand empathy as weakness in order to protect that very freedom.

In addition to these popular cultural currents, there exists a far less known but intellectually more influential source of a new cruelty: the movement identified by {% cite gebru:2024 %} as the "TESCREAL bundle." This acronym combines seven ideological currents that currently dominate Silicon Valley:

+ Transhumanism (technological self-optimization)
+ Extropianism (unlimited expansion)
+ Singularitarianism (belief in technological singularity)
+ Cosmism (colonization of space)
+ Rationalism[^1] (perfection of thinking)
+ Effective Altruism (maximum ethical efficiency)
+ Longtermism

[^1]: To be understood here in the sense of community platforms like LessWrong, not as classical philosophical rationalism.

The authors define the bundle not merely as a loose list, but as a coherent normative movement with deep roots in the Anglo-American eugenics tradition of the 20th century. Their goal is a new utopia: the creation of an artificial superintelligence as a tool for digital immortality and galactic colonization.

>We believe any deceleration of AI will cost lives. Deaths that were preventable by the AI that was prevented from existing is a form of murder. -- Marc Andreessen (The Techno-Optimist Manifesto)

The danger of this bundle lies in the abstraction of suffering. While the followers of these movements warn of hypothetical apocalyptic scenarios of the distant (and now closer) future under the guise of AI safety (not AI ethics), they evade responsibility for currently observable harms such as obvious discrimination, precarious work in data annotation, accountability for data theft, the rising cost of many consumer goods (a kind of indirect AI tax), or the impending explosion of resource consumption in the face of a coming climate catastrophe.

A form of cruelty arises that is mathematically calculated against the future. In this radical utilitarian worldview, present human lives become negligible rounding errors in a cosmic calculation that is willing to gamble real humanity for the sake of an imagined posthuman successor society. This is why climate change, interestingly, hardly appears as a threat in these circles, because it can be assumed that it does not mean a spectacular end for everyone. This also leads to extremely strange arguments or "thought experiments", such as the comparison between a person being tortured for years and the grains of sand that irritate a huge number of people. Or the "logical" conclusion that the world's resources must be put into people in highly developed countries, because they are after all much more productive.

What unites all these groups---however different their articulations may be in detail---is a deep belief in a *teleological narrative of progress* and the superiority of the stronger, whether in physical, intellectual, economic, or technological terms. It is the continuation of that ancient heritage in which Plato already indissolubly linked the True with the Good. Behind it lies the hope that a rational truth---in the form of a superintelligence that is hardly distinguishable from a god---might triumph over "mere" emotional narratives and the imponderables of interpersonal stories; the longing that a technized morality could ultimately provide us with an infallible calculation of what is right.

However, this belief is paired with a deep despair that leads into cynicism. Only those who consider humans to be intellectually inferior and thus live with a diffuse self-hatred will wish to overcome them in this technical form. If Nietzsche still wanted to dance around the abyss, this group wants to fill it at the expense of life. In this worldview, the archaic urge to transcend the boundaries of human finitude reveals itself. It is the promise of a world that is intended to enable an elite and arrogant minority to create a reality that finally frees them from the fear of their own transience.

In the face of this *renaissance of cruelty*, there is a need for the pragmatic and radically self-reflective thoughts of two (or maybe three) figures who never met personally or intellectually during their lives. And yet Richard Rorty and Humberto Maturana share a profound commonality that makes them sharp critics of current dehumanization: they attempt to save the human by bringing it down from heaven to earth; by acknowledging its limitations but at the same time enjoying them as something entirely magnificent.

*Solidarity* arises in both through a great humility that will come as a surprise as an argument at this point: both bid farewell to objectivity as we usually understand it and view evolution as a radically *contingent* process, so that our existence and our history do not emerge from any dialectical dynamic that works towards something absolute.

It may seem paradoxical that precisely the deconstruction of objectivity should open a path to less cruelty. Are we not currently in deep social crises precisely because the media spread of misinformation undermines the common ground? If we give up objectivity as the final arbiter, do we not open the doors wide to charlatans for whom any means is right to prevent a consensus?

>At the heart of the dispute between philosophy and poetry lies the fear that the imagination goes all the way to the bottom—that there is nothing we talk about that we could not also have talked about differently. This fear leads philosophers to be almost obsessed with the necessity of gaining direct access to reality. 'Direct' in this case means unmediated by language—for we are aware with a certain unease that our language could well have been a completely different one. -- {% cite rorty:2016 %}

These fears are justified, yet they overlook the liberating effect of this loss. As long as we believe that truth is "out there" independent of us, we can hide behind it. We can claim that our actions are merely the rational execution of indisputable facts. But when we set aside objectivity in the classical sense, a question arises that truly brings us back together as a community: how can we then even agree on anything? How does what we mistake for objectivity arise?

When we accept that we can no longer represent the world in a supposedly god-like truthfulness, this loss brings us out of our epistemological loneliness. It becomes clear that the world in which we live is not a finished exhibit that we view passively, but a fragile fabric that we have created together. Without the anchor of an external truth, we are forced to turn to one another. Responsibility for the world no longer rests on "things", but on our relationships. In this new vulnerability lies the root of solidarity: we must agree, not because reality dictates it, but because otherwise we have no common world.

If we attempt to negotiate empathy and solidarity on a rational basis and at the same time assume that "objectivity is the delusion of an observer who assumes that observations can be made without an observer" {% cite foerster:2006 %}, then we run the risk of losing sight of cruelty and humiliations because we rely on an instance outside. Incompleteness will enable those with influence and means to place their goals above others because they can always invent a language that legitimizes what they hope for.

## Truth in Brackets

Rorty argued vehemently in his time against a metaphysical realism, that is, the notion he called the "mirror of nature" {% cite rorty:1979 %}. It is the idea that our language could more or less accurately represent the world. He thus criticized the classical *correspondence theory of truth*, which postulates that truth is a correspondence between language and an objective, consciousness-independent reality. His central counter-argument is: Truth is a property of sentences. The world itself, however, possesses no language; it cannot make our sentences true.

>The world does not speak. Only we do. The world can, after we have programmed ourselves with a language, lead us to hold beliefs. But it cannot suggest a language to us in which we should speak." -- {% cite rorty:1989 %}

As a pragmatist, however, Rorty refused to "prove" his position in the analytical sense, as this would already be a concession to the opponent's meta-system. Following {% cite wittgenstein:1953 %}, he understands analytical philosophy as a language game with strict internal rules---comparable to geometry, in which theorems are proven on the basis of agreed-upon axioms. Metaphysical realists, however, commit a category error: they attempt to use the rules of the "objectivity game" to prove that this game is the only true representation of reality.

Rorty's crucial point is the circularity of this justification: one cannot use the internal logic of a vocabulary to legitimize that very vocabulary against all alternatives. The choice of a way of speaking is not a logical, but an intuitive, creative, or simply useful decision. We do not "prove" pragmatism; we simply state that it is more helpful for our current goals---such as the reduction of suffering---than metaphysics. As an anti-representationalist, Rorty is not interested in winning the debate on the realist's terms; he wants, as he often put it, to "change the subject". He merely provides pragmatic arguments, i.e., reasons why it would be better to give up the distinction between appearance and reality {% cite rorty:2016 %}.

This turn away from foundationalism---the architectural metaphor of knowledge as a building on an unshakable foundation---is replaced by Rorty with the metaphor of the web. This step is rooted in Quinean holism {% cite quine:1951 %}. In such a web, every belief is held not by a foundation, but by its functional connection to all other threads. Anyone who demands proof for the entire web is seeking a "God's-eye view" outside of our linguistic practice---a standpoint that is fundamentally inaccessible to us. There is no "outside" of our conversations; the development of our culture is an ongoing holistic redescription, driven by the hope for a more coherent form of life.

>We should not understand terms like 'gravity' or 'human rights' as entities whose essence remains mysteriously hidden, but as sounds and signs through whose use more significant and better social practices became possible. Intellectual and moral progress is not an approximation of a preceding goal, but the surpassing of the past. What we call "improved knowledge" should not be interpreted as better access to the real, but as an increased ability to do things. [...] Freedom begins when we can discuss which words better describe a situation. Knowledge and freedom develop simultaneously. -- {% cite rorty:2016 %}

Whenever one makes a distinction in one's experience or one's life, one makes this distinction in the realm in which it appears through language. When we learn to speak, we immerse ourselves in an existing network that we as individuals did not invent. An irreversible split occurs between world and sign, a kind of doubling of the world. In this sense, we do not just learn a language, but language, as a cultural achievement, learns us.

To understand Rorty's position, one must move away from the idea that truth is an essence to be discovered. One must move away from the idea that language arose to talk about things that one already knew but for which one did not yet have words. One must consider how language might have arisen. Maturana will also help us here.

>According to the conception of human abilities that I propose here, the use of persuasion instead of violence is an innovation comparable to the beaver's dam-building. Like the cooperation of the beavers in building the dam, this too is a social practice. It was initiated by the novel suggestion that we could use noises instead of physical coercion to move other people to cooperate. This suggestion brought forth language. Rationality, thinking, and cognition all began simultaneously with language. Language does not begin by humans giving names to things they had already thought about, but by proto-humans using noises in innovative ways---just as proto-beavers established the practice of dam-building by using sticks and mud in innovative ways. -- {% cite rorty:2016 %}

For Rorty, truth is therefore not a goal that lies outside of our linguistic practices, but simply a complementary predicate that we bestow upon those beliefs that have proven themselves in practice. He replaces the metaphysical concept of truth with that of "*justified assertibility*". Truth is therefore what we consider to be consensus---capable and useful for our common goals in free and open conversation among equals. We use the word "true" to agree with a statement, to mark arguments as justified, or to express that we are initially assuming something that can later be replaced with more useful descriptions or vocabularies. For Rorty, truth is thus not a correspondence with a hard reality, but a form of intersubjective agreement. It is a relational property.

When we say a belief is true, we are actually only saying that we currently have no better reasons for an alternative. We always say this in a community of speakers. This radical relativization is, however, not a departure from life. We do not give up on wanting to understand the world better because of this inclusion in language. On the contrary: when truth is no longer dictated by a "world in itself", the entire responsibility for what we consider true and good lies with us and our community. Of course, this can lead to great uncertainty, for not even the truthful can lead us through the paths of our very personal experiences.

Nietzsche already pointed to a forgetting of this all-too-human groundlessness:

>Only through the forgetting of that primitive world of metaphors, only through the hardening and stiffening of an original mass of images flowing forth from the primal power of human imagination in heated liquid, only through the invincible belief that this sun, this window, this table is a truth in itself, in short, only by the fact that man forgets himself as a subject, and specifically as an artistically creative subject, does he live with some peace, security, and consistency: if he could for just a moment step out of the prison walls of this belief, it would be over with his 'self-consciousness' immediately. -- {% cite nietzsche:1873 %}

For traditional philosophy, this stance smells of relativism or irrationalism. Rorty counters, however, that this is simply a consistent ethnocentrism: we speak from within the liberal tradition. Our "rigor" is not a discovery of eternal laws, but the result of conversational habits that have proven themselves for the expansion of freedom and the reduction of cruelty.

Kant attempted to formulate universal principles that should apply to all, and thus created the basis for solidarity, human rights, and democracy. But his reason could not order the surplus of meaning that arose through the newly won freedom: too many opinions, too many perspectives, too much criticism and deconstruction. Kant overlooked the obvious: that he himself as an observer must remain blind to his observing. We are free but also overwhelmed, that is arguably the dilemma of liberal democracy: we do not know what to do and yet we must decide.

Nietzsche already taught us that no description of the world is necessary---that we could always tell other stories to understand ourselves and our community differently. Kant, on the other hand, reminds us that such new creations have limits where they overlook the suffering of others. Rorty recognizes in this tension the productive condition of a liberal modernity: without Nietzsche no renewal, without Kant no consideration. We need Nietzsche so that life remains interesting, and Kant so that it remains bearable.

If truth can no longer function as the "Good in itself" (in Plato's sense), Rorty as a liberal thinker must re-found morality. He can no longer prove solidarity as a rational duty (Kant) or as a metaphysical necessity. He can also not justify it utilitarily. He must refrain from presupposing the human as a being that is rational in and of itself. He adopts the definition of Judith Shklar and defines the Liberal as 

>someone for whom cruelty is the worst thing one can do.

He does not try to prove that cruelty is objectively wrong (since the world speaks no moral language). Instead, he states that our liberal culture has agreed that the avoidance of suffering is the highest goal.

In Rorty's ironic culture, it can therefore no longer be about finding the right words or a common reality behind the appearances that will connect people. He gives up the dream of finding the True, Good, and Beautiful for everyone. Truth for him is a property of sentences, and sentences are made by humans. Therefore, we should stop asking: "Does my belief correspond to true reality?". It is more important to ask: "Does this belief help us live together better, more freely, and less cruelly?"

In his utopia, solidarity is not viewed as a fact that must be recognized by removing prejudices or uncovering previously hidden depths. Uncovering becomes impossible as soon as we give up the search for a common essence or nature, for then the effort for a universal justification is in vain. Solidarity must therefore be produced.

Thus, Rorty's liberals set solidarity as their goal. This goal is achieved not through reason or any logical proofs, but through imagination and empathy---through the imaginative ability to see strangers as fellow sufferers. Solidarity is not discovered through reflection; it is created. It is created by increasing our sensitivity to the particular subtleties of the pain and humiliation of other, unknown people through literature, novels, personal conversations, films, theater, music, (video) games, and reports. Such increased sensitivity makes it difficult to marginalize people who are different from us by assuming: they would not feel as we do, or there will always be suffering, so why should one not let them suffer? It is a process of emotional expansion of the "we", not of epistemological insight. This process of seeing other people as "one of us" instead of "the others" consists of describing in detail what unknown people are like and in redescribing who we ourselves are and how we recognize each other {% cite rorty:2016 %}.

Rorty did not believe that a person becomes more moral by reading Kant or learning syllogisms. Moral insight for him is not a matter of the intellect, but of the imagination. We act morally because we have learned to put ourselves in the pain of others. Because our mothers and hopefully also fathers explained to us: "We don't do that. We respect each other. We don't hit but discuss. We acknowledge the fragility of the other. We care for each other. We see in every human being an invaluable value." A novel about the suffering of a slave or a report about the conditions in a textile factory does more for morality than any philosophical work. These narratives expand our ability to see in the stranger someone who is "one of us".

We do not have to prove our moral values in order to passionately stand up for them. We defend them because they are ours and because they enable a world in which less pain exists. If we no longer share this conviction, however, then we will not get any further with debates, thought experiments, and strict arguments.

At this point, {% cite rorty:1989 %} introduces the concept of the *liberal ironist*---a figure who considers cruelty the worst "evil" and at the same time knows about the fundamental contingency of her own values (like freedom and empathy). The ironist understands that her vocabularies could have turned out quite differently had history proceeded only slightly differently or had she adopted a different vocabulary from her mother. But Rorty also warns urgently of the cruelty that arises when ironists attempt to impose their private vocabulary on others:

>There is something potentially very cruel about the claim [that the language people speak is a lottery for ironists]. For the most effective way to inflict lasting pain on people is to humiliate them by making everything that seemed particularly important to them appear vain, obsolete, powerless. -- {% cite rorty:1989 %}

Rorty therefore calls for a strict separation between private self-creation and public coexistence. While he admires *ironic theorists* like Nietzsche, Heidegger, or Derrida for their ability for radical redescription, he warns against their tendency to absolutize the autonomy of the individual at the expense of the general public. In his admiration for these thinkers, Rorty at the same time recognizes their possessive danger: Nietzsche, in his love for self-overcoming, was hardly concerned with reducing suffering, but rather with enabling a spiritual elite to reinvent Europe. In this sense, Rorty argues that Nietzsche's vocabulary can unfold its power in the private space of self-stylization, but in the discourse of an open society it produces little that is fruitful—or even something destructive. When the artwork that we are ourselves stifles the self-creation of others, a deep cruelty arises. Autonomy and self-efficacy are ideals that even liberals revere, but in the shadow of these ideals lurks the terrifying connection between art and torture. A vocabulary is not per se "the good" or "the beautiful"; it can be the tool of a humiliation that silences the other.

## Biology of Cognition

At this point, we can build a bridge to our second protagonist; for Humberto Maturana was not only a biologist but a cybernetic thinker who approached the question of "how we and other organisms perceive" from a biological perspective.

>The question of reality is neither a physical nor a philosophical one, but a biological one, for it is a question of life. -- Humberto Maturana

It can be argued that he thereby roused cybernetics from its mechanistic infancy—a historical development that receives little attention in computer science and AI research today and, in my opinion, requires correction.

While Rorty paints the picture of a self-contained web of language, {% cite maturana:1980 %} presents us with an equally closed system through the biology of cognition: our nervous system. His central thesis is that the nervous system is *operatively closed* and therefore, in principle, cannot represent anything that is "out there", as no information from "out there" enters the system. It does not mirror an external world but reacts exclusively to internal changes of state.

In this reading, the *system* is merely perturbed (stimulated) by its *environment*. This means that information is by no means transferred like a commodity from the outside in; it is constructed within the system {% cite maturana:1987 %}. Shannon's information theory, which forms the foundation of computer science, only works because two technical devices are constructed in such a way that they process perturbations so that the same information is constructed. In computer science, we call this encoding. A nervous system, however, was not built; it is the result of an extremely long evolutionary drift in permanent co-evolution with its environment {% cite maturana:1987 %}.

To clarify this dynamic, Maturana makes a strict distinction between *organization* and *structure*: The organization of a system (for example, a bicycle) refers to those relations between components that constitute its identity. If I change the tire, the identity of the bicycle remains intact.

In the case of a living organism, this organization is *autopoiesis*---that is, the continuous process of self-production and self-maintenance. As long as autopoiesis exists, the living being is a living being. If autopoiesis ends, the organism becomes something else.

Autopoiesis means that operations produce and reproduce their own basis of existence, maintaining the organization by continually changing the structure of the organism. Structure represents the concrete (in this case, material) realization. In every moment, our material realization changes, but we remain an identity as long as our autopoiesis (which is what we are) continues.

Interestingly, a structure can simultaneously be part of different organizations---analogous to a chair that functions as part of a table in a different functional context. Or let us consider an example within our own bodies: Structurally, a bone (e.g., the femur) is a hard structure made of calcium phosphate crystals and collagen fibers. For the musculoskeletal system, it functions as a mechanical lever and a static support element. The relations are of a physical nature (pressure resistance, attachment point for tendons). Yet, inside the same bone lies the marrow. Here, the bone is part of the immune system and blood formation. The relations here are biochemical and cellular.

Through the concept of *structural determinism*, Maturana explains how operatively closed systems can interact without a direct, instructive flow of information taking place. When a person runs, massive mechanical forces act upon the femur. According to conventional understanding, one might say the muscle "informs" the bone about the load, whereupon the bone remains stable or strengthens over the long term. For Maturana, however, no instructive flow of information occurs here. The muscle does not "tell" the bone what to do. Rather, the mechanical pressure represents a perturbation (a disturbance). How the bone reacts to this pressure is determined exclusively by its current structure. If the bone is structurally weakened by osteoporosis, it will break under the load. If it is healthy and mineralized, it will absorb the energy elastically. The external stimulus (the pressure) determines that something happens, but the structure of the bone determines what happens.

In its simultaneous function as part of the blood-forming system, this determinism becomes even clearer: the same mechanical pressure that stresses the bone as a lever in no way "instructs" the bone marrow to produce more red blood cells. If the marrow reacts, it does so only because the mechanical vibration triggers a structural change in the tissue that evokes its own reaction within the autopoietic organization of blood formation.

Although systems depend causally on their environment and can even be destroyed by it, "causality" here (with the exception of destruction) does not mean external determination. How a system reacts to an external stimulus is determined solely by its own structure. If I press a key on my keyboard, the internal wiring of the computer determines what effect this has. My finger merely provides the necessary perturbation but does not determine the result. In the event of a computer malfunction, no one would think to look for the cause in the user's finger.

With this analysis, Maturana inevitably leads his theory back to its origin: the observer. All cognition is the cognition of an observer {% cite maturana:1985 %}, though only--as we shall see momentarily---if we, as listeners or understanders, allow the question of the observer. If we do not allow it, we take the observer's abilities as given—as properties of the observer that require no further explanation or reflection.

If we assume a world independent of us, we follow the path of "objectivity-without-parentheses" {% cite maturana:1988 %}. Even the everyday language of science---for instance, when we say: "I can measure *it* with my instrument"---implies this stance. The vocabulary suggests a separation: the object exists out there, autonomous and independent of the act of measuring or of the observer themselves.

If, however, we accept the question of the observer, this forces us into an epistemological recursion. We must now describe a mechanism that explains not only the result of an observation but observing itself. Since observing is now understood as an active process while we simultaneously attempt to make this process experiential, a mutual interpenetration arises: the explanation of the process and the execution of the process can interfere with or modify each other.

Maturana consistently seeks this explanatory mechanism in the domain of experience. The goal is a mechanism whose result is the existence of the observer themselves. Since the observer is a living system, this search leads inevitably into biological dynamics. Every disturbance of the biological integrity of the system is at once a disturbance of observing; one cannot "interfere" with biology without interfering with cognition itself.

This journey ultimately leads us back to language as the medium in which these biological coordinations take place. According to Maturana, it enables a *structural coupling* between human beings as well as between humans and the world. At this point, Rorty and Maturana meet once again. Language is not merely a vehicle for the transfer of information, but the field in which we coordinately bring forth our shared world. Maturana goes so far as to claim that it was only through language that the being we designate as human emerged. He also frequently speaks of "languaging beings" to emphasize that language is a doing.

When two living systems (e.g., two humans) interact with each other repeatedly, they begin to mutually influence their structures. Since both are structurally determined, one system does not "instruct" the other, but they perturb (disturb/stimulate) each other. When these interactions become stable, Maturana speaks of a *consensual domain*.

First, we coordinate our behavior. A simple example: a proto-human points to a berry, and another looks. This is a simple coordination of actions. No language is taking place yet, but rather a shared acting in the here and now. Language only emerges when we begin to *coordinate our coordinations*. This sounds abstract but can be understood as follows: we use signs (sounds, gestures) no longer just to react to things in the world, but to react to our shared behavior.

>Language is the recursive coordination of coordinative actions -- Maturana

If I do not just point to the berry but make a sound that means: "Don't look at this berry, but at the other one", then we are coordinating how we coordinate our attention. We step out of pure action into a domain where action itself becomes the subject.

In this moment, the objective world (objectivity-without-parentheses) "extinguishes" and we begin to bring forth a world together ("bringing forth a world"). The world of which we speak is the result of this constant coupling {% cite maturana:2000 %}. For Maturana, being human is inseparably linked to this process: we exist as humans in language and thus on the path of "objectivity-in-parentheses".

This expression functions as a warning sign: it marks the insight that we possess no cognitive access to refer to a reality independent of us. We are always bound to the conditions of our own biological existence.

It becomes clear that Maturana understands the question of the observer not as a marginal note, but as the fundamental condition of every theory. In doing so, he draws upon George Spencer-Brown's *Laws of Form* {% cite brown:1969 %}---that attempt to trace mathematics back to a foundation that begins even before arithmetic or logic: the act of distinction.

In this logic, distinction is the necessary primal act that must precede all pointing and all mathematizing. Observing, therefore, means to draw a distinction and to mark one side of the resulting boundary. A name can only arise where that which is designated is distinguished from that which is not designated; language only becomes tangible where we demarcate a word against the background of the non-word (or of silence).

Observing is thus not a passive mirroring of a pre-existing reality, but an active, constitutive process of sense-construction. The Spencer-Brownian imperative is: "Draw a distinction!" Everything that is observed is inseparably linked to the observer who draws this boundary. In this sense, cognition is not a reflection, but a creation through distinction.

Maturana radicalizes the departure from objective truth by shifting the focus from things to relations and from objects to processes. For him, an explanation is never a mere statement about how the world *is*, but a reformulation of experience. He points out that a process or mechanism only becomes an "explanation" when a listener accepts it as such. An explanation is therefore not a *solipsistic event*, but a deeply interpersonal phenomenon:

>Explanations are not explanations in and of themselves, but they have to do with human relations. [...] They are like the pacifiers given to babies. They are pacifiers in human relations. -- Maturana

In this image, the scientific or philosophical explanation loses its status as a sacred inscription of nature. Instead, it becomes an instrument of coexistence. We offer explanations to generate certainty of expectation and to find consensus within the shared space of "languaging" (linguistic action). An explanation is accepted according to the individual criteria of the listener—it must "fit", like the pacifier to the infant, rather than being "correct". Maturana essentially draws our attention to something so obvious that we tend to forget it precisely because it is what is closest to us.

Whatever we explain as living systems are ultimately not facts of an external world, but always the structures of our own experience. In our current culture, however, there prevails a deeply rooted habit of speaking as if the phenomena we explain, or which provoke our questions, existed *in and of themselves*---that is, completely independent of us as observers.

Maturana breaks radically with this illusion of objectivity. He claims that even scientific explanations are, at their core, nothing more than the systematic explanation of the scientists' own experiences.

>Explanations are reformulations of experiences that we wish to explain through a process that produces these experiences as a consequence. -- Maturana

When we say in a scientific context: "If you perform that experiment under these conditions, you will see that result", we are actually describing instructions for the coordination of experiences. The fundamental error of our culture consists in interpreting the result of this process as something separate from our observations---as an objective truth that was "out there" waiting to be discovered. In reality, however, we bring forth the world of which we speak in the very moment of speaking and acting.

To illustrate this idea of the reformulation of experience, let us consider the simple question: "Why is the sky blue in the morning?" On one hand, we could accept a mythological explanation: every morning, God takes a brush in his hand and repaints the firmament. This is a valid explanatory mechanism provided the listener recognizes the underlying elements---"brush", "paint", "the movement of a hand"---as familiar components of their experiential world. Here, the explanation works through an analogy to craftsmanship.

On the other hand, there is the physical explanation: sunlight enters the Earth's atmosphere and interacts with the particles found there. Depending on the wavelength of the light and the composition of the atmosphere, the light is scattered such that primarily the blue portion of the spectrum reaches our eye.

Maturana's decisive insight is this: the scientific explanation also uses terms like "particle", "light", or "wavelength", which ultimately are nothing more than highly refined and instrumentally mediated elements of our experience. The scientist does not explain the "world in itself"; rather, they link experiences (measurement data, observations of light refraction) together in such a way that they make the experience of the blue sky appear as a necessary consequence.

Both explanations are "pacifiers" within a specific social or intellectual context. The difference does not lie in one being "true" and the other "false" in the sense of a correspondence to reality. The difference lies in the criteria of validation and in the kind of world they bring forth. While one explanation supports a world of divine intervention, the other enables a world of technological predictability and manipulation.

A scientific explanation gains its power by proposing a mechanism formulated so precisely that any other observer who performs the same actions must necessarily have the same experience. It is about the maximum coordination of actions. If I calculate the wavelength of light, I can build lasers, lay fiber-optic cables, and run the internet. The scientific explanation "wins" because it enables a far more complex and stable structural coupling between us and our environment than the narrative of God's paintbrush.

Similarly, for Rorty, science is a very "useful tool", because a vocabulary that speaks of light waves and particles allows us to make predictions and cure diseases. It is a vocabulary that helps us reduce suffering and shape the world according to our desires. Science prevails because it grants us greater agency. In a liberal society, we also prefer it because it is based on public verifiability rather than private revelation—it is thus "more democratic", as it invites everyone to perform the experiments themselves.

Both of these thinkers essentially want to tell us that the success of science is often misunderstood. Science is merely a very good tool for handling the world. If we use this tool to dismiss empathy as "illogical" or "weak", we commit a category error. We are using a tool designed for describing particles to decide on the value of human beings. When we confuse the effectiveness of a tool with a moral guideline, we risk forgetting the very foundation upon which our ability to survive is based.

## Biology of Love and Solidarity of Culture

Maturana argues that while we are *operatively closed* systems, we can only survive as such if we form social systems. In this context, for Maturana, the "social" is not a vague cultural superstructure, but a precise biological domain. Accordingly, a social system arises exclusively through a single biological dynamic: *love*.

>Love is not a feeling, but a biological phenomenon. It is the acceptance of the other as a legitimate other in coexistence. -- Maturana

In this sense, empathy is not a "weakness", as Musk and his disciples claim. Nor is it a strength; rather, it is the biological condition for the possibility of social community {% cite maturana:2008 %}. Without the biological readiness to accept the other—not as an obstacle or a tool, but as a legitimate partner in the coordination of behavior—there would be no *languaging*: that continuous weaving of a shared world through language.

Maturana takes this a step further: since human consciousness and the perception of an "I" only emerge within language, and language in turn presupposes the social, a radical insight follows: Without love, there is no I. We exist as individuals only because we are embedded in a domain of acceptance. The *glorification of cruelty* thus proves to be a fundamental biological error. It is not an expression of strength or a form of evolutionary progress, but a process of biological decay. Whoever negates empathy destroys the structural conditions under which human life and thought became possible in the first place. They terminate the social process not just morally, but functionally.

If this is so, why do we kill one another, and why does cruelty prevail in our world? To understand this, one must grasp evolution as a process in which living systems and their medium change together by following the path of recurrent interactions. In this *evolutionary drift*, mutual structural congruence (adaptation) is maintained only as long as the system's way of life realizes the conditions of its own preservation.

When living in language--oral *languaging*---began to be systemically preserved across generations in our primate lineage about three million years ago, our entire biology was reshaped around this process. Our nervous system, our face, the larynx, and our ways of relating evolved in structural coupling to this "being-in-language". We do not *use* language; we *exist within it*. Our humanity is inseparably interwoven with the relational dimensions of our linguistic being.

Within this framework, emotions can be characterized as dynamic bodily dispositions for specific ways of relating. Love is that domain through which the "other" arises as a legitimate other in coexistence with us. Aggression, by contrast, is the domain through which the legitimacy of the other is denied; fear, in turn, leads to withdrawal from the circumstances. From this perspective, love is simply the biological dynamic that constitutes trust and mutual acceptance in relationships of proximity and intimacy.

We humans are loving animals---or we still are. Love is the bedrock of our existence; perhaps our species should therefore more accurately be called *Homo sapiens amans*. Everything we do in life, we ultimately do to obtain love: we want to emerge as legitimate beings who do not have to justify their existence. We enjoy cooperation and community; we suffer under competition and coercion. In this context, love is the first and most powerful medicine; it creates the conditions for the bodily dynamics of self-healing.

Particularly significant for the current debate on artificial and human intelligence is Maturana's observation that intelligence is not a purely cognitive computational feat. Since intelligence is linked to the acceptance of the legitimacy of the other and the expansion of possibilities for consensuality, love is the only emotion that expands intelligence. Emotions based on the negation of the other---such as ambition, competitiveness, or aggression---reduce intelligence, as they narrow the domain of possible cooperation. Love, by contrast, is visionary.

We will only remain *Homo sapiens amans* as long as love remains the central emotion of our systemic identity. If we surrender ourselves to aggression, we risk the transition to *Homo sapiens aggressans*---a way of life that could end in mutual destruction or ecological blindness. Love does not have to be learned; it is our natural state and our biological foundation. Aggression, on the other hand, must be laboriously cultivated.

The systematic denial of love through rational arguments---using terms such as efficiency, profitability, progress, or perfection---is a carefully cultivated feature of our patriarchal culture and the technocratic movements of our time. Yet this denial of love is the source of all human suffering; it is an addiction to self-righteousness that ultimately undermines humanity itself at a systemic level.

If love is the acceptance of the "other" as a legitimate other, then for Maturana, hatred is its exact biological opposite: it is the active denial of the legitimacy of the other in coexistence. In hatred, the counterpart ceases to be a co-observer or a partner in *languaging*; they are reduced to an object, an obstacle, or an enemy.

Maturana emphasizes that we are not born as hateful beings, but that hatred is a cultural practice. It is a disposition for action maintained through specific *systems of conversation*. In this reading, war is not a biological destiny, but a cultural phenomenon based on a systematic denial of love.

War can only exist where human biology is "numbed" by rational arguments. Maturana points out that we use rational systems---ideologies of purity, progress, national security, or economic necessity---to override the biological revulsion toward the destruction of life. In a war, one no longer "sees" the other; one sees only the vocabulary that marks them as "scum", "traitor", or "resource obstacle".

This is the point where Maturana's analysis of aggression directly intersects with today's manosphere and the TESCREAL movement. When Elon Musk or the proponents of Longtermism rationalize suffering as a "necessary sacrifice" for a galactic future, they are constructing exactly that kind of blindness that Maturana describes as the foundation of war. They create a vocabulary that denies the legitimacy of the currently suffering human being.

A social system based on hatred or competition is, for Maturana, essentially a contradiction in terms. Since social systems are constituted by the biology of love, warlike or hateful interactions are, strictly speaking, antisocial processes. They utilize the mechanisms of human coupling (language, coordination) to destroy the very goal of that coupling: the mutual preservation of life.

War is thus the ultimate form of ecological blindness. It is the attempt to secure one's own existence by destroying the very domain in which one first emerged as a human being. Maturana warns us: we cannot deny the other without simultaneously destroying a part of our own humanity. Every bullet fired in the name of a "higher truth" destroys not only a body but also the consensual space that defines us as *Homo sapiens amans*.

Maturana thus tends to understand humans as beings-in-language who are fundamentally loving---as *Homo sapiens amans*---and to view culture as a kind of corruption. War, aggression, and the "new cruelty" are, for him, cultural pathologies. They are "systems of conversation" that we have invented to deny our biological nature. *Patriarchal culture* uses rationality as a tool to numb natural empathy. This leads to the conclusion that we must declutter our culture to be allowed to be loving animals once again.

Rorty, on the other hand, is a decisive anti-essentialist. For him, there is no "good core" and no "true nature" upon which we could rely. The human being is a blank slate. We are neither good nor evil; we are contingent. In a pre-liberal state, we tend to be cruel, selfish, and tribalistic (*hitting*). The fact that we today (sometimes) prefer talking to hitting one another is not an "awakening of our nature", but a magnificent invention. Since there is no "true" human nature to which we could appeal (no divine law, no biological superiority), solidarity must be created. The creation of solidarity is an utterly contingent phenomenon---from our perspective, an extremely fortunate and innovative event.

Both, however, agree that solidarity does not arise through rational insight into any mathematical truths. Rather, it is the precondition for our capacity for rationality. It has evolved into the ability to view more and more differences between us (skin color, gender, ideology) as unimportant compared to what we have in common—namely, the insight that we are all *capable of suffering*.

For Rorty, solidarity is the project of continually expanding the "we-feeling" by broadening our sensitivity to the cruelty and humiliations experienced by the other; in other words, by seeing more. For him, cruelty is the inability to recognize the pain of the other as real. Such a sensitization does not occur through correct thinking, but through the experience of stories and narratives. It is possible for us because we are capable of *imagining*.

Whether one says that cruelty is a "denial of biology" (Maturana) or a "betrayal of our best cultural vocabulary" (Rorty)—in either case, the new cruelty is unmasked for what it is: a project hostile to life.

## Systemic Blindness

At this point, a certain chilliness enters the fray that puts Maturana and Rorty in their place; for while Maturana the biologist can offer insights into the dynamics of organisms, and Rorty the liberal philosopher can provide pragmatic arguments for liberalism, {% cite luhmann:1984 luhmann:1998 %} reveals why the humanist resistance just formulated underestimates the operative momentum of modern social systems.

First of all, {% cite luhmann:1985 %} radicalizes Maturana's idea of operative closure and structural coupling by consistently assuming a structural coupling between psychic systems (consciousness) and social systems. In other words, he posits an operative boundary between organisms that can never directly exchange with one another. No thought enters communication, and no word enters the mind. For him, this leads to a dehumanization of social systems {% cite moeller:2011 %}, as these systems become blind to the biological suffering of the human being. As a biological creature, the human being is merely *environment* for the social system.

This explains the observed "abstraction of suffering" in a structural way: functional systems of modernity—such as the economy, technology, or science—operate according to binary codes (e.g., profit/loss or true/untrue). These systems are simply blind to *perturbations* that do not fit into their specific code. When Elon Musk discredits empathy as a "means to suicide", he is speaking from the pure logic of a functionally differentiated system that regards human emotion as mere noise in the environment.

From a Luhmannian perspective, the "new cruelty" is thus not a moral failure of individual actors, but rather a symptom of the increased autonomy of technical and economic systems. These systems have loosened their *structural coupling* to the feeling consciousness of the individual to such an extent that moral appeals for solidarity no longer reach them at all. In Luhmann's view, solidarity becomes a "mere semantics"---a beautiful narrative of the past that no longer finds any resonance in the hard operative reality of algorithms.

>In the 19th century, given the dramatic nature of changes in social structure, this idea was abandoned and replaced by the purely moral postulate of solidarity. As is well known, Durkheim still believed that this solidarity would have to establish itself almost automatically, forced by differentiation, but at the same time should be demanded by sociology as a form of morality. In the 20th century, this notion had to be replaced by the demand for a politico-economic equalization of the living conditions of all people, to be brought about by a politically active society. At the end of this century, it can hardly be denied that people will have to cope with a society without happiness, without solidarity, and without the prospect of equalizing living conditions. -- {% cite luhmann:1995b %}

Thus, Luhmann radicalizes the challenge for Rorty and Maturana: if the systems of our world are structurally blind to love, then the struggle for a new vocabulary of sensitivity (Rorty) and the return to the biology of love (Maturana) is no mere philosophical desideratum. It is the desperate attempt at a "re-entry"---the re-introduction of the human being into a system that has long since degraded them to a mere data point in its environment.

## Evolution knows no Progress

Another central aspect in the thinking of both Maturana and Luhmann is the consistent rejection of the conventional notion of progress. Progress, they argues, is inevitably tied to a goal or a measurable ideal---a concept that has no place in biology. Maturana does not conceive of evolution as an ascent toward ever "better" or "higher" forms, but rather as an *evolutionary drift*.

Like a raft, we drift structurally upon the ocean for as long as it takes for the waves to tear the raft apart. During this journey, the structures of the raft constantly change through structural coupling, provided the organization of the raft as such is maintained. Everything that is not destroyed is alive and is thus *perfectly* adapted to its environment. In this view, environment and system form a unity of distinction---a "perfect continence" {% cite brown:1969 %}. This means: an environment only exists as an environment for a specific system, and vice versa.

From this follows a radical equality of all life that shatters our deep-seated anthropocentrism. From a biological perspective, a bacterium is not "less advanced" than a human being. Both exist in the here and now; both have successfully endured the billion-year chain of selection. There is no such thing as "better adapted"; adaptation is a binary condition of survival, not a linear optimization process. Evolution is not working toward anything; it is not an engineer who improves, but an ongoing process of selection through structural coupling.

This point unmasks the hubris of the "new cruelty". When tech ideologues claim that humans must be replaced or augmented by a Superintelligence (AGI) because it is "more advanced" or "more intelligent", they are committing a category error. An Artificial General Intelligence---provided it operates stably as a system---would merely be another form of structural coupling, another participant in the evolutionary drift. It would not stand on a higher rung of an imaginary ladder, for there is no ladder. It would simply be a different kind of raft on a different ocean. The belief that AGI is the teleological goal of evolution is nothing more than a secularized theology that elevates a technical system to a new god.

>Evolution is like a blind wanderer moving through the landscape, not because he has a goal, but because with every step he preserves the ground beneath his feet. -- following Maturana

That the history of life appears to us in retrospect as a chain of "improvements" is a pure interpretation of the observer. We look at the filtered results of the past and project an intention where only a mechanical process of filtering took place. For Maturana, life is not a striving for perfection, but the continuous maintenance of autopoietic organization in the here and now. Anyone who today offsets human lives against an imagined post-human future is sacrificing real existence for a statistical fiction based on a misunderstood concept of evolution.

In a striking parallel to Maturana's biological drift, Richard Rorty also rejects the notion that human history is a necessary process of convergence toward an "objective truth" or a "true human essence". For Rorty, there is no Progress with a capital "P"---no teleological movement and no dialectic of history toward a goal prescribed by nature or God; no Absolute Reason unfolding itself in history.

Instead, the development toward liberal democracies and increased sensitivity is not a law of nature, but a fortunate accident of history. It could have turned out differently, and it can always be otherwise. 
At the end of his lecture, Rorty addresses the new freedom found in contingency in order to once again emphasize pragmatic caution:

>If we can avoid the fallacy of thjnking either that the contingency of a social practice implies that it should be dropped or that the sheer novelty of a suggested practice is sufficient reason to adopt it, then intellectual life will survive the collapse of the apparence-reality distinction, as well as the relativization of reationality. -- {% cite rorty:2016 %}

This corresponds exactly with Maturana's drift: we are here because certain social practices of cooperation and the avoidance of pain have proven viable thus far, not because they are "truer" than cruelty. Both thinkers emphasize the radical contingency of our existence. If we have no metaphysical guarantee for our progress, then we have nothing left to lean on—except ourselves and the decision to minimize cruelty because we have chosen to do so, not because a cosmic law demands it.

## Conclusion

Once we become aware of the outrageous luck we have had in building such a---for the most part—wonderful world; once we simultaneously realize that we are in the process of *defeating* our own environment and thus our basis for existence, the realization becomes unavoidable: the era of patriarchal dominance and rationalized aggression is over.

We thus stand at an evolutionary crossroads. Niklas Luhmann's systems theory shows us exactly how high the stakes truly are. His analysis of functional differentiation reveals a society whose subsystems—the economy, technology, the algorithms of Silicon Valley---have long since achieved a chilled autonomy. These systems are not "evil" per se, but they are structurally blind to human suffering; they operate in an operative closure that has degraded the human being to an irrelevant environment.

Luhmann's supposed hopelessness is, in truth, a radical wake-up call: systems will not develop empathy on their own. An algorithm cannot love. If we do not actively work to force a resonance for the human through Rorty's *sentimental education* and new vocabularies of solidarity, we risk the emergence of a world that functions perfectly, but in which no one is at home anymore.

Perhaps what Rorty, in his wise foresight, repeatedly pointed out to us is our imagination. He emphasized the power of novels, stories, and narratives, placing them even before reason. 

>Rationality is a matter of making allowed moves within a language game. Imagination creates the games that reason proceeds to play. [...] Reason cannot get outside the latest circle that imagination has drawn. It is in this sense, and only in this sense, that imagination holds the primacy. -- {% cite rorty:2016 %}

Yet, when we turn our gaze toward the TESCREAL bundle, their ideas seem hopelessly unimaginative. Technical innovations that lead to disruption and the dream of superintelligence have been around for a long time; we read such ideas in ancient sci-fi books. It may sound spectacular, but are difficult problems actually being solved here, or is technology not exactly the excuse for no longer having to exert the imagination at all?

>If we can avoid the fallacy of thjnking either that the contingency of a social practice implies that it should be dropped or that the sheer novelty of a suggested practice is sufficient reason to adopt it, then intellectual life will survive the collapse of the apparence-reality distinction, as well as the relativization of reationality. -- Ruha Benjamin

The choice does not lie with "us" in the sense that "we" could avert or control what happens.
But it is also clear that social or technical systems would collapse without the human being, and that our perturbations can trigger new, surprising dynamics---we simply cannot foresee them.
Imagination remains our most important ability.

>Given everything humanity has accomplished, all of it driven by leaps of the imagination, why is envisioning a safer, saner, happier, more peaceful path forward so consistently beyond our reach? Indeed, why does it seems increasingly beyond our reach? [...] Even among people who work within the creative industries, their imagination seems increasingly harnessed to create demand for things nobody really needs, whose production is increasingly pushing our human and ecological systems to the brink of collapse---almost as if imagionation has been coopted in the service of our own extinction. -- {% cite hopkins:2021 %}

Either we surrender to the systemic cold and the resulting *Homo sapiens aggressans*, or we recall that primal biological force that Maturana describes so aptly. We must readjust the structural coupling between our systems and our biology of love. However, I do not believe we will achieve this through another attempt at dominance.

If we can no longer determine how our thoughts irritate communication---if we observe that the dynamics of systems cannot be controlled---then perhaps an anticipatory retreat is appropriate to achieve a slowing down---a kind of *poetic care*. By such care, we understand a practice that no longer misunderstands the technical as an instrument of dominion, but guards it as a medium for encountering the world. It is careful in its awareness of the fragility of systemic balances and poetic in its courage not to stifle the *unverfügbar* (the "unavailable" or "uncontrollable") through control. Where objectifying thought reduces everything to unambiguousness, poetic care preserves the ambiguity of the imaginative.

The raft on which we drift has become fragile. We can no longer stabilize it through hardness, but only through the softness of cooperation. In the end, there remains the simple but radical insight: we will survive as loving beings---or not at all. Let us hope that the new aggressions and cruelties will reveal themselves as the final death throes of an old world passing away.
